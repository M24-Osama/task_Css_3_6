Rainforests, often referred to as the “lungs of the Earth,” play a crucial role in maintaining global climate stability and biodiversity. Covering only about 6% of the Earth’s surface, these lush green havens are home to more than half of the world’s plant and animal species. The Amazon rainforest, the largest tropical rainforest on the planet, is a prime example of nature’s abundance. It is estimated to house approximately 390 billion individual trees belonging to around 16,000 different species. The rainforest’s dense canopy provides a habitat for countless creatures, from colorful birds and insects to elusive jaguars and agile monkeys. These ecosystems are also rich in plant life, with many species possessing medicinal properties that have been used by indigenous communities for centuries. However, rainforests are under constant threat from deforestation, driven by logging, agriculture, and urbanization, which poses a significant risk to the myriad species that call these forests home.
